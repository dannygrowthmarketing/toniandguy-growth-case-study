The growth engine, written as a formula.

six pillars · one system

A salon's colour formula isn't a recipe. It is a precise prescription — base, tone, glaze, finish — written in proportion, calibrated to the head it'll sit on. The growth engine was built the same way: six pillars, six proportions, one composition that compounds.

Method

The base sets the brief; the bond holds it; the finish is where everyone else stops measuring. Each pillar was instrumented as its own P&L. No channel survived without justifying its share. Proportions were re-mixed monthly off Looker reads.

Six pillars, applied in proportion across 16 sites. The same composition, re-tuned per store.

112+ creatives tested. Postcode-level targeting per store. PMax for booking intent, Meta for awareness and retargeting at location level. Behind it: a unified CRM that turned 16 sets of bookings into one record, and a Looker Studio dashboard that ran the weekly executive brief faster than the meeting that used to consume it.

Folio V
V.
Marginalia

Notes pencilled in the margin — for the next marketer.

distilled from 16 months

A craft book is most useful where the previous owner wrote in it. Four annotations — each one a hard-earned correction to a default assumption. Take them, ignore them, but don't pretend they weren't there.

i.
averages lie. ask per-store.

Granularity beats averages.

Treating 16 stores as one campaign hides winners and losers. Per-store spend revealed where to refresh creative versus where to shift budget. A city-wide average would have hidden the fact that the bottom eight stores needed entirely different creative and budget logic than the flagships.

ii.
work the creative, not the bid.

CTR leads, CPC follows.

Once creative–audience fit improved (CTR up 5×), CPC collapsed. Google rewards relevance with cheaper auctions. The work was not on bids. It was on understanding which creative, which audience, which offer, at which store, at which moment — then systematising that understanding across 112+ creatives.

iii.
retention is leverage. always.

Retention beats acquisition.

Cutting churn from 27% to 7% was worth more than any single paid optimisation. Every percentage point of retention compounds forward. The acquisition engine was filling a bucket with a hole in the bottom; we plugged the hole first.

iv.
CRM = priority #1. don't argue.

CRM is infrastructure, not a tool.

Without centralised data, every channel flies blind. CRM is priority #1 in any multi-location B2C. The HubSpot/Zoho implementation was the unlock for everything else — segmentation, lead scoring, lifecycle automation, attribution, and the weekly executive brief that ran decisions faster than the meeting that used to consume them.
